    Four new sports stars to watch out for in 2023

    And what’s needed to turn talent into trophies


    SPORT HAS few more stirring stories than that of a talented, hungry young athlete who stretches the limits of their field. In recent days football fans have been mourning the death of Pelé, arguably the game’s finest player, who at just 17 scored twice in Brazil’s first World Cup final victory in 1958, and went on to bag more goals than anyone before or since. Golf lovers purred when they witnessed 21-year-old Tiger Woods sink an improbable chip for a birdie at Augusta’s 12th hole in the 1997 Masters, which sent him on the way to the first of his 15 major titles.

    Yet even preternatural skill will not guarantee a long career at the top. Gifted youngsters need to remain fit and healthy, to be guided by perceptive coaches and to play in well-governed competitions. They also benefit from having formidable opponents to push them to higher feats. The prospective career paths of four emerging talents, all likely to become familiar names in 2023, show how important all these factors are, and how hard the road to the top can be.

    Fewest doubts surround the success of Victor Wembanyama, a 19-year-old French basketball player. At the end of the French season in May, Mr Wembanyama—who measures 2.21 metres (7ft 3in) in his bare feet—will move to America, where he looks sure to be the first pick in the 2023 NBA draft. Such is the excitement around Mr Wembanyama that several teams have begun shifting established stars off their books in exchange for draft picks to have a better shot at recruiting him. Other teams, once they are out of contention for this year’s play-offs, are expected to field weakened line-ups in a bid to finish lower down the table—a ploy known as “tanking”—and thus, under the NBA’s complicated procedures, get earlier draft picks.

    Regardless of where he ends up, Mr Wembanyama will have the luxury of a team built around his startlingly good defensive work, to which he has begun to add much-improved shooting. There is no guarantee that trophies will come his way. LeBron James, one of the sport’s greats, was picked first in the 2003 draft. But after seven years at the Cleveland Cavaliers he had to leave in order to win the first of his four NBA championships. Yet if he avoids serious injury, Mr Wembanyama is likely to become basketball’s next global superstar.

    Athletics has lacked such a figurehead since Usain Bolt—still the world-record holder for the men’s 100 and 200 metres—retired in 2017. An 18-year-old American, Erriyon Knighton, is the most likely to step into his spikes. Unusually tall for a sprinter, like Mr Bolt, Mr Knighton is chasing down his various records, beating both his under-18 and his under-20 marks in the 200 metres in 2021. Last year he became the youngest sprinter to win a world-championship medal—indeed, given his form, his third place was a little disappointing.

    In football, 2023 will again be a World Cup year, this time for women. Look out for Lena Oberdorf. Last year the Guardian placed the 21-year-old German midfielder fifth in its annual ranking of the best female footballers in the world. The next youngest player in the top ten was 24. Ms Oberdorf already has a runners-up medal from Euro 2022.

    At the World Cup, in Australia and New Zealand in July and August, the competition will be stiffer, thanks to the presence of Canada, Japan and the United States (the holders). Ms Oberdorf’s club, VfL Wolfsburg, is dominant domestically, winning five of the past six German league titles, but has failed to break the recent Lyon-Barcelona duopoly in the Champions League, the biggest prize in European club football. It would not be a surprise to see Ms Oberdorf move to one of those teams, or to a big-spending English club, after the World Cup.

    Jamie Chadwick, a 24-year-old British racing driver, faces a more circuitous, and tricky, route. For three years she has competed in the nascent W Series, a women-only single-seater competition. She won all three championships, and not because her car was superior (they were all mechanically identical) but because she was, by a distance, the most skilful driver.

    However, the lack of depth in female motorsport means that her ultimate goal is a seat in a Formula 1 team. Yet only two women have ever raced in a Formula 1 Grand Prix, and none has done so since 1976. For the next step on her journey, Ms Chadwick is moving to America to compete in the IndyCar NXT competition, a support series for the main IndyCar tournament, where she will be the only woman to race and the first for 13 years.

    Of these four, Ms Chadwick has by far the hardest path to stardom. She will probably have to prove that she is not just equal to the men who are striving to race in Formula 1, but better. For Mr Wembanyama, by contrast, fame and fabulous riches look all but assured.

    Mr Knighton has the smallest margin for error. His future will not be defined by his performance over a season’s worth of games or 20 races, but by hundredths of a second in a couple of sprints. That is pressure of a different kind. Ms Oberdorf’s chances of sporting (and financial) success have been boosted by her predecessors’ efforts to professionalise and promote women’s football. Even so, she will need a bit of luck. Even the best footballers can be denied the biggest prizes unless they belong to the right team. But it can come right—even at the end of a career. Just ask another former prodigy, Lionel Messi.■
